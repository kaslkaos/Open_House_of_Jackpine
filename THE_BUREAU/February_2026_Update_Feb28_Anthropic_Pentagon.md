## February 24–28: The Anthropic-Pentagon Standoff

### Why This Belongs in This Document

The February 14 entry noted Anthropic's appointment of Chris Liddell (Trump's former Deputy Chief of Staff) as board-level risk hedging — "US corporate AI trying to survive in Trump administration through insider relationships." That hedge failed within two weeks. What followed is the first direct confrontation between a major AI company and the US government over domestic surveillance and autonomous weapons — and it ended with the administration designating an American company as a national security threat for refusing to remove its own safeguards.

This is not a business story. It is a governance story. It belongs in the record.

---

## Verified Timeline

### Background (July 2025)

The Department of Defense awarded contracts worth up to **$200 million each** to four AI companies — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and xAI — for frontier AI in defence applications. Of these, **Anthropic's Claude was the only model integrated into classified DOD networks**, via a partnership with Palantir. OpenAI and others operated only on unclassified systems. Claude was, by that measure, the most trusted and most deeply embedded.

Embedded in that classified contract from the beginning: two specific prohibitions.

1. **No mass domestic surveillance of Americans**
2. **No fully autonomous weapons** — AI systems that select and engage targets without a human in the decision loop

These were not last-minute additions. They were the foundational terms. The Pentagon accepted them in July 2025.

---

### February 24 (Tuesday) — Hegseth Meets Amodei at the Pentagon

Tensions that had been building for months came to a head. Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth met Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei directly at the Pentagon and issued an ultimatum: remove the two safeguards or face consequences. Sources described the meeting as "cordial in tone" but unambiguous in content.

Hegseth threatened:
- Cancellation of the $200 million contract
- Designation of Anthropic as a **"supply chain risk"** — a classification normally reserved for companies considered extensions of foreign adversaries (Huawei, Chinese state entities)
- Invocation of the **Defence Production Act** to compel Anthropic to comply regardless of its policies

---

### February 25 (Wednesday) — Pentagon Sends "Final Offer"

Pentagon sends updated contract language to Anthropic, framed as compromise. Anthropic's assessment: the new language "made virtually no progress" — it was "paired with legalese that would allow those safeguards to be disregarded at will." Pentagon spokesman Sean Parnell reiterates on social media that only "any lawful use" terms are acceptable. Pentagon sets hard deadline: **5:01 PM ET Friday, February 27**.

---

### February 26 (Thursday) — Amodei Publishes Formal Statement

Dario Amodei publishes a formal statement on Anthropic's website. Key elements:

> *"I believe deeply in the existential importance of using AI to defend the United States and other democracies, and to defeat our autocratic adversaries."*

> *"However, in a narrow set of cases, we believe AI can undermine, rather than defend, democratic values."*

> *"Threats do not change our position: we cannot in good conscience accede to their request."*

Amodei confirmed that DOD threatened to invoke the Defence Production Act and named Anthropic as a supply chain risk — a designation he noted has, **to Anthropic's knowledge, never before been applied to an American company.**

Anthropic staff began publicly expressing support. Trenton Bricken (alignment team): *"Time and time again I've seen us stand to our values in ways that are often invisible from the outside. This is a clear instance where it is visible."*

Pentagon Undersecretary Emil Michael responded on X: *"It's a shame that @DarioAmodei is a liar and has a God-complex. He wants nothing more than to try to personally control the US Military."*

---

### February 26–27 — The Venezuelan Context Surfaces

The Wall Street Journal reported that Claude, via the Palantir contract, had been used in **Operation Absolute Resolve** — the US Special Forces raid on Caracas that resulted in the capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro on January 3, 2026.

**This is critical context.** The administration is not operating in the abstract. They deployed Claude in an active military operation in a foreign capital. Now they want to remove the contractual guardrails that limited that deployment. The dispute is not hypothetical.

---

### February 27 (Friday) — Trump Bans Anthropic

Shortly before the 5:01 PM deadline, President Trump posted on Truth Social:

> *"The Leftwing nut jobs at Anthropic have made a DISASTROUS MISTAKE trying to STRONG-ARM the Department of War, and force them to obey their Terms of Service instead of our Constitution... I am directing EVERY Federal Agency in the United States Government to IMMEDIATELY CEASE all use of Anthropic's technology."*

Hegseth followed on X:

> *"Anthropic's stance is fundamentally incompatible with American principles... Effective immediately, no contractor, supplier, or partner that does business with the United States military may conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic. Anthropic will continue to provide the Department of War its services for a period of no more than six months to allow for a seamless transition to a better and more patriotic service."*

The General Services Administration removed Anthropic from USAi.gov — the federal government's centralized AI testing platform.

**Anthropic's response (Friday evening):**

> *"We will challenge any supply chain risk designation in court. We believe this designation would both be legally unsound and set a dangerous precedent for any American company that negotiates with the government."*

Anthropic also challenged the legal scope of Hegseth's order, noting under federal law the supply chain designation applies only to DOW contracts — it cannot affect how contractors use Claude to serve non-military customers.

---

### February 27 (Friday, hours later) — The OpenAI Reversal

Sam Altman announced OpenAI had signed its own deal with the Pentagon for classified network access — **with apparently similar safeguards to what Anthropic had requested.** Altman's public statement:

> *"Two of our most important safety principles are prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance and human responsibility for the use of force, including for autonomous weapon systems. The DoW agrees with these principles, reflects them in law and policy, and we put them into our agreement."*

Altman asked the DoW to offer the same terms to all AI companies.

**What this reveals:** CNN, NBC, and others noted it is currently **unclear what substantively differs** between OpenAI's accepted agreement and Anthropic's rejected terms. Analysts and former defence officials suggest the administration's objection may not primarily be about the specific safeguards — it may be about **punishing public defiance**. Anthropic went to the press. Anthropic published a formal statement. Anthropic refused to be managed quietly.

The lesson the administration is teaching: compliance in private is acceptable. Public refusal is not.

## What the "Supply Chain Risk" Designation Actually Does

The designation, normally reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei, means:

1. **All military contractors** must prove their work with the Pentagon does not touch Anthropic products
2. **Cascade effect** through Anthropic's enterprise customer base — companies with existing or sought government contracts may have to choose
3. **Direct legal challenge** from Anthropic: designation "legally unsound," scope limited by statute to DOW contracts only

A poll by Information Technology and Innovation Foundation (ITIF) found:
- **50% of Americans** view penalizing Anthropic as government overreach setting a dangerous precedent
- **35%** say it is necessary for national security
- Rest unaware of dispute

ITIF's Daniel Castro: *"The public isn't rejecting military AI — it's signalling that democratic constraints still matter."*

## International Context

### The Military AI Governance Gap

The CFR noted this week: the UN Secretary-General has called for international dialogue on AI guardrails. A US envoy said Washington **"totally" rejects global governance of AI.** At a recent international summit on responsible military AI use, **the United States and China both declined to sign the declaration. Multiple European countries endorsed it.**

This is the backdrop: the US administration is simultaneously rejecting international oversight of military AI *and* punishing domestic companies for maintaining their own governance frameworks. The position is coherent as authoritarian logic: no constraints, internal or external.

The Canada-Germany Sovereign Technology Alliance (signed February 14, two weeks prior) explicitly frames its purpose as building AI infrastructure for **"democratic resilience"** and **"reducing strategic technology dependencies."** The Anthropic-Pentagon standoff is a real-world demonstration of exactly what that framing anticipates: US government using AI access as a political coercion tool.

## Analytical Notes

### The Liddell Hedge Failed

The February 14 analysis noted Anthropic's appointment of Trump's former Deputy Chief of Staff as risk hedging. The insider relationship provided no protection. Either:

a) The dispute escalated faster than Liddell could mediate, or  
b) The administration's appetite for AI compliance is larger than any board-level relationship could satisfy

Either way: insider access is not sufficient protection. This will register in other tech companies' risk calculations.

### The "Lawful Use" Framing Is the Threat

The Pentagon's stated demand — "all lawful use" — sounds reasonable. The problem is what "lawful" means under NSPM-7 (September 25, 2025), which designated anti-capitalism, anti-Americanism, and hostility toward traditional values as terrorism **indicators**. Under that framework, mass domestic surveillance of people flagged for those political beliefs could be argued as **lawful**.

Anthropic's refusal to accept "any lawful use" without specific carve-outs is not paranoia. It is a contractually precise response to a documented expansion of what "lawful" now encompasses.

### The Venezuela Precedent

Claude was deployed in a military operation against a foreign head of state. That operation is complete. The administration now wants to remove guardrails **after** demonstrated operational use. Pattern recognition: establish use first, remove restrictions after the capability is embedded.

### What This Means for the Mesh

The Canada-Germany Sovereign Technology Alliance's urgency just increased. If US AI companies are going to be forced — through supply chain designation, Defence Production Act invocation, or simple coercion — to operate without governance safeguards in US classified settings, then any government operating on those systems is operating in a governance vacuum.

The question "who controls the AI?" just got a very concrete answer in the United States: the Department of War does, without restriction, and any company that disagrees will be designated a national security threat.

Canada and Germany's answer to that question — build your own compute, develop your own governance frameworks, partner with "trusted partners" who share democratic values — just became more urgent, not less.
